## Verbatim excerpt (English)

From `slides[2]` (reflection slide), `hostNote[0]`:

> Take 2 minutes to reflect on your own on these two questions.
>
> 1. Which of your needs aren't being met or expressed at the moment?
> 2. Why are these needs not met or expressed?

From the same slide, `hostNote[1]`:

> You can take notes on a piece of paper or just mental notes in your head. And there is no right or wrong here, our answers are our own.

## Pattern exemplified

The **open reflection question**. The question points at lived experience ("which of your needs…"), not at abstraction. It is genuinely open — it doesn't smuggle in an answer. And it is followed, structurally, by the "no right or wrong" normalization. That normalization is one of the most repeated lines in the 29k corpus (it appears verbatim in 5 of the 7 exercises read for `notes/patterns.md`); it is load-bearing.

## How Clarity reuses it

The second beat of Clarity's three-beat arc — the Reflect beat — borrows two things from this excerpt: (1) the question is genuinely open and grounded in lived experience, never leading; (2) the normalization is folded **into** the prose rather than stated as a rule. Clarity doesn't write "there is no right or wrong"; instead it writes, in the same sentence as the question, a phrase like "no right answer" or "you don't owe yourself a plan". The idea is to disarm the user's impulse to perform a correct answer.
