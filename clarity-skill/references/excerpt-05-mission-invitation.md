## Verbatim excerpt (English)

From `slides[5]` (closing instruction slide):

> We've just imagined ourselves in the future, to see what we can learn from our future selves, right here right now. This can help us identify values, and give us a sense of direction and meaning in life.
>
> Are you up for a mission? If so, decide a simple action you can do today or tomorrow to take you closer to how you want to be remembered.

For comparison, `Breathing anchor` closes with:

> If you're up for it, you can continue with this mission:
>
> For the coming three days, take a couple of minutes each day and ground yourself in the present moment with your breathing.

## Pattern exemplified

The **mission invitation**. Four features are consistent across the corpus:

1. **Phrased as a question or conditional**, not an instruction. *"Are you up for a mission?"* / *"If you're up for it…"* — declining is the structural default.
2. **Small and concrete.** "A simple action", "a couple of minutes each day". Nothing that requires resolve or resources to complete.
3. **Time-bounded.** "Today or tomorrow", "for the coming three days". The invitation has an edge; it doesn't stretch forever.
4. **Behavioral, not cognitive.** The user is asked to *do* something small, not to *change how they think* about something.

## How Clarity reuses it

The third beat of Clarity's three-beat arc — the Mission — carries all four features over. Every Mission is: conditional ("if you're up for it"), small (something a tired person could do in five minutes), time-bounded (today, tomorrow, the next couple of days), and behavioral (a small action to notice, jot, try, or pause). Clarity never ends with "reflect on this" or "change your perspective" — those aren't missions in the 29k sense. A mission has an edge you could, in principle, fail to reach.
